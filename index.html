Research Highlights

Machine learning for wireless networks

Can machine learning help us in handling the increasing complexity of current wireless networks and find the best operation setting for a wireless network? Our recent works answer this question affirmatively. 


See our solution aiOS @IEEE/IFIP NOMS'20 for a WLAN and DeepCTC for cross-technology communication and DeepTxFinder@ICCCN20 for multiple transmitter localization in a wireless network. Our recent paper@ComMag21 discusses the need for a unified framework for integrating machine-learning tools into a software-defined WLAN. You can find also the abstract of our invited tutorial @Crowncom 2017.

Networking with the edge

Increasing number of end-user devices and the volume of traffic generated by and to these devices lead to many challenges for the network providers which have already been challenged by massive increase in network traffic. For users, cloud-based systems pose a privacy risk as the user's data is constantly communicated to the cloud. Motivated by these issues (and others), edge paradigm aims at bringing communication and computation closer to the user so that  above-mentioned issues can be mitigated to some extent. However, many questions remain yet to be addressed.


See our vision for an intelligent container overlay ICON@HotNets18, ExEC@EdgeSys19, and using the power of the edge devices for a distributed edge cloud DeCloud@ICDCS19, and our research @EdgeSys'20 on when edge provides the highest performance for IoT data analytics functions as compared to cloud placement.  Our paper@HotNets20 revisits the arguments motivating the edge with a large-scale measurement campaign and discusses the promise of edge for various applications. Our paper EdgeDASH@TNSM proposes to use the edge caches for improving the DASH streaming performance for multiple clients competing for a bottleneck link's capacity. We survey the literature on pervasive edge computing for IIoT in our article@IEEE Access.

LTE-WiFi coexistence in unlicensed bands

In unlicensed LTE, LTE operators can use unlicensed 5 GHz spectrum along with their licensed spectrum via carrier aggregation techniques. However, they first have to implement coexistence schemes with the incumbents of unlicensed spectrum, most importantly with WiFi. A straighforward approach is to leave some resource blocks for WiFi's use: frequency, time, space. Our idea is to exploit interference nulling to increase the concurrency of LTE-U and WiFi transmissions via coexistence gaps in space domain. LTE-U BS with multiple antennas can use some of its antenna diversity to null its interference on neighboring WiFi nodes and thereby enabling their downlink traffic. We introduce the idea and analyse its performance under various LTE-WiFi distance, number of WiFi users, and number of LTE-U antennas. Our analysis reveal up to 221% gain in LTE throughput and 44% in that of WiFi.

See our paper presented at IEEE WoWMoM. Also, you can check our solution Xzero presented at European Wireless 2018 and demo-ed at MobiSys 2018, extended version at Null-While-Talk at PMC. See our papers on cross-technology communication, CTC-CEM@WoWMoM'20, Punched-cards-over-the-air @WoWMoM'20 (best paper award), Demo@INFOCOM'20 (best demo award), and DeepCTC.

Name Resolution in ICN

We argue that most of the benefits of an NRS can be realized by indexing only a small fraction of the requested contents benefiting from the NRS the most. While larger NRS provides slightly higher cache hits for small caches, the impact is more visible for larger cache capacity. In contrast to diminishing gains in cache hit, data latency decreases further with increasing NRS size owing to faster name resolution.

See our paper presented at ACM ICN16. Also, other ICN related works of ours can be found here: scoped-flooding@JSAC and Prodiluvian@ACM ICN15 (best paper award).

Spass: sensing as a service via smart contracts

Mobile network operators (MNO) can opportunistically use the licensed bands of the primary users (PU) provided that they monitor the spectrum and stop their transmission upon detection of the PU. As deploying spectrum sensors may be prohibitively expensive, the MNO can buy spectrum sensing service from sensing helpers in its proximity. However, such a trade requires a framework with three key functions: helper selection, faulty or malicious helper identification, and payment to honest helpers. Here, we introduce Spass which provides these functions and facilitates a fair exchange between the entities without a trusted third party via smart contracts (SC) running on a blockchain network. Via simulations, we show under which conditions Spass-powered service leads to a profitable business for an MNO.

See our paper presented at IEEE DYSPAN 2018 and extended version published at IEEE TCCN. You can find also the slides of my talk @Aalto-CS.
