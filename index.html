EERI: Energy-efficient and resilient Internet

The Internet forms the backbone of the world's most crucial services, ranging from education to healthcare and from transportation to communications. Given its critical role in the continuity of everyday services, it is paramount to understand and improve the resilience of the internet. Besides its resilience, energy consumption of the internet is of great concern; it accounts for 10 percent of global energy consumption, which is expected to rise to more than 20 percent by 2030. With increasing dependency on digital services, improving energy efficiency at all levels - from hardware to software and from simple IoT devices to sophisticated network infrastructures- becomes paramount to decrease the energy footprint of the internet and to increase its resilience against possible energy outages. In this context, EERI aims at both understanding and improving the energy efficiency and resilience of the internet. We will design energy-efficient hardware and control, resource allocation algorithms considering both the wired and wireless communication networks with optimization strategies that make the internet resilient against failures. Please visit the project website for more up-to-date information. Here is our faculty video having a short overview of EERI.

Our EERI papers are: impact of multi-connectivity on network capacity (appearing at IEEE TVT), analysis of resilience of cellular networks (under review), analysis of the benefits offered by national roaming in terms of resilience, coverage, capacity, and transmission power consumption (IFIP Networking Workshop IOCRCI 2023), BEAM-ALIGN (under review) , impact of power consumption models on NOMA performance (under review), and power allocation for NOMA (EUCNC'23).

Related events we organized: Resilience of communication networks, two sessions at Risk&Resilience Festival 2022.

Networking with the edge

Increasing number of end-user devices and the volume of traffic generated by and to these devices lead to many challenges for the network providers which have already been challenged by massive increase in network traffic. For users, cloud-based systems pose a privacy risk as the user's data is constantly communicated to the cloud. Motivated by these issues (and others), edge paradigm aims at bringing communication and computation closer to the user so that  above-mentioned issues can be mitigated to some extent. However, many questions remain yet to be addressed.


See our vision for an intelligent container overlay ICON@HotNets18, ExEC@EdgeSys19, and using the power of the edge devices for a distributed edge cloud DeCloud@ICDCS19, and our research @EdgeSys'20 on when edge provides the highest performance for IoT data analytics functions as compared to cloud placement.  Our paper@HotNets20 revisits the arguments motivating the edge with a large-scale measurement campaign and discusses the promise of edge for various applications. Our paper EdgeDASH@TNSM proposes to use the edge caches for improving the DASH streaming performance for multiple clients competing for a bottleneck link's capacity. We survey the literature on pervasive edge computing for IIoT in our article@IEEE Access.

Related events we organized: Lorentz 2021 (online) workshop and Lorentz 2023 workshop.

Dynamic spectrum sharing and coexistence in unlicensed spectrum

In unlicensed LTE, LTE operators can use unlicensed 5 GHz spectrum along with their licensed spectrum via carrier aggregation techniques. However, they first have to implement coexistence schemes with the incumbents of unlicensed spectrum, most importantly with WiFi. A straighforward approach is to leave some resource blocks for WiFi's use: frequency, time, space. Our idea is to exploit interference nulling to increase the concurrency of LTE-U and WiFi transmissions via coexistence gaps in space domain. LTE-U BS with multiple antennas can use some of its antenna diversity to null its interference on neighboring WiFi nodes and thereby enabling their downlink traffic. We introduce the idea and analyse its performance under various LTE-WiFi distance, number of WiFi users, and number of LTE-U antennas. Our analysis reveal up to 221% gain in LTE throughput and 44% in that of WiFi.

See our paper presented at IEEE WoWMoM. Also, you can check our solution Xzero presented at European Wireless 2018 and demo-ed at MobiSys 2018, extended version at Null-While-Talk at PMC. See our papers on cross-technology communication, CTC-CEM@WoWMoM'20, Punched-cards-over-the-air@WoWMoM'20 (best paper award), Demo@INFOCOM'20 (best demo award), and DeepCTC. Spectrum sensing by the help of crowd sensors could help identifying the state of the spectrum at scale. See our paper presented at IEEE DYSPAN 2018 and extended version published at IEEE TCCN. You can find also the slides of my talk @Aalto-CS.
