Research

LTE-WiFi coexistence in unlicensed bands

In unlicensed LTE, LTE operators can use unlicensed 5 GHz spectrum along with their licensed spectrum via carrier aggregation techniques. However, they first have to implement coexistence schemes with the incumbents of unlicensed spectrum, most importantly with WiFi. A straighforward approach is to leave some resource blocks for WiFi's use: frequency, time, space. Our idea is to exploit interference nulling to increase the concurrency of LTE-U and WiFi transmissions via coexistence gaps in space domain. LTE-U BS with multiple antennas can use some of its antenna diversity to null its interference on neighboring WiFi nodes and thereby enabling their downlink traffic. We introduce the idea and analyse its performance under various LTE-WiFi distance, number of WiFi users, and number of LTE-U antennas. Our analysis reveal up to 221% gain in LTE throughput and 44% in that of WiFi.

See our paper presented at IEEE WoWMoM.
See our solution Xzero presented at European Wireless 2018 and demo-ed at MobiSys 2018.

User-AP Mapping for Enterprise WLANs

We argue that in an enterprise WLAN we can leverage the centralized controller by equipping it with a periodic user-AP association decision logic to increase the network efficiency and fairness among users. Our analysis via simulations on realistic scenarios-- conference, office, and shopping mall, shows the superior performance of our proposals in terms of aggregate logarithmic throughput. While the utility gain over the conventional approach is modest, up to 18 percent, the resulting increase in the weakest user's throughput is significant~(71-120 percent) as well as that of AP load balance and fairness of user throughputs. The highest performance is observed in conference setting where the users are unevenly distributed in the network and hence there is a huge load imbalance among the APs.

See our paper presented at ACM MSWIM 2017.

Name Resolution in ICN

We argue that most of the benefits of an NRS can be realized by indexing only a small fraction of the requested contents benefiting from the NRS the most. While larger NRS provides slightly higher cache hits for small caches, the impact is more visible for larger cache capacity. In contrast to diminishing gains in cache hit, data latency decreases further with increasing NRS size owing to faster name resolution.

See our paper presented at ACM ICN 2016.

White Spaces for Sustainable Data Offloading

Mobile data offloading is a method to move traffic from the cellular network through other means, such as WiFi and small cells. While network providers have applied offloading very successfully, e.g., 51% of mobile data traffic was offloaded onto fixed networks in 2015, we need more sustainable solutions. In particular, our argument is that without changing the way how we share the data “road” to a more efficient one, the solutions will only be shortlived. Moreover, we need to exploit the user mobility and short-range communications for content delivery. With this in mind, we introduce white space offloading which uses the spectrum that is licensed but remains unoccupied as well as mobile user contacts among nodes to deliver content. Using our measurements from Google Spectrum Database and simulations, we show the additional offloading capacity facilitated by white spaces.

See our paper presented at CrownCom 2016.
